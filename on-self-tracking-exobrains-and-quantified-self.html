On self-tracking, exobrains and quantified self

Sep 28th 2020 By Vyacheslav Basharov Updated on: Sep 28th 2020

Beyond fitness tracking and note-taking. 

But what happens when you start taking an active interest in your own data?

Can you learn more about yourself than you would have by simply experiencing things? 

What if you think of your life as a system than can be optimised? 

Back in 2012, Stephen Wolfram published an essay titled "The Personal Analytics of My Life", exploring the vast amounts of data he collected about himself from early 1990's to the early 2010's. Emails, phone calls, footsteps, keystrokes. Yes, keyloggers aren't just for hackers, it turns out. Using this data, Stephen asks questions like "how many hours have I spent on the phone each day", or "how has my vocabulary evolved over the years". 

Stephen has his own digital infrastructure - a set of tools that he built, combined with software and hardware that has deeply integrated into his life and work. And being a scientist, Stephen 

It's fascinating how the perception towards personal data can shift when you take an active role and ownership of it, in order to understand your life better, make changes and optimise for a certain goal. 

It's fascinating to see how many insights can be extracted from this data - information which many people would not even consider collecting. 

It's a very scientific, evidence-based approach to looking at your life.
